Why this exists

Noise reduction is the paywall that hurts.

Of everything DaVinci Resolve locks behind the Studio license, noise reduction hits filmmakers hardest.

You can edit in the free version. You can grade in it. But shoot in low light — which is to say, make an independent film — and you hit the wall: the NR palette is Studio-only. And noise isn't cosmetic. Color correction and VFX need a clean foundation — every contrast move amplifies grain, every key edges against it, every composite inherits it.

Here's the thing: you don't need AI for this. Good noise reduction is robust statistics, careful signal processing, and honest engineering — plus the will to not charge for it. Hush gets you Studio-class results, free, with something even the paid tools don't offer: total transparency into what it's measuring and doing.

How it works

Five steps. Click each one.

1Measure3 estimators × 16 brightness bands
2Temporalhard-knee gate, zero ghosting
3Spatialself-truing NLM + blotch pass
4Refinesat curve · texture · film grain
5Inspectthe viewer becomes the scope

Three estimators measure the noise of every frame: median frame-to-frame difference (immune to the correlated, blotchy noise compression creates), plus fine and coarse Laplacian statistics — with a separate estimate for every brightness band, because shadows are noisier than highlights. Robust medians keep edges, texture and motion out of the measurement.

Transparency is the feature

See what it sees.

Resolve's built-in NR is a black box: sliders in, pixels out. Hush renders its measurements into the viewer — because a tool this consequential to your image shouldn't require faith.

For the technically curious

The math, briefly.

Measuring noise without a chart

Everything is calibrated in units of the noise standard deviation σ. The primary estimator for video is the frame you just watched — on static pixels, frame difference is pure noise with spread √2σ, regardless of spatial correlation:

σ_T = median(|I_t − I_t±1|) / (0.6745 · √2)

Motion robustness: the unbiased median and 20th-percentile estimates agree on static footage; motion inflates the median first, so disagreement means "trust only the static quantile." Spatially, Immerkær's operator (response std = 6σ, rejects gradients) runs at two scales — and per brightness band, using a 35th percentile so a band full of real texture can't fake a high reading:

σ_fine = median(|L ∗ I|) / (6 · 0.6745) L = [1 −2 1; −2 4 −2; 1 −2 1] gain(band) = 1 + w · (σ_band/σ_ref − 1) w = n/(n + 2000) (confidence)

A temporal gate that cannot ghost

w = 1 − smoothstep(1.13σ_T, 1.13σ_T + k·σ_T, |Δpatch|)

Full weight while a difference is explainable by noise; exactly zero past the knee. An earlier version used a Gaussian falloff — its fat tail blended pixels several σ apart at 30–40% weight and turned slow pans into soup. The hard knee ended that structurally, and a slow-drift regression test now makes ghosting a build failure.

Spatial filtering that measures its own inheritance

Compression correlates noise across frames, so temporal averaging removes less than √N theory promises. Hush re-measures the residual on the merged image itself and tunes non-local means to that measurement — filter strength h = 1.15σ_residual for luma, 2.2σ for chroma, patch distances bias-corrected by the 2σ² noise alone contributes, plus a sparse 16-px luma-guided pass for chroma blotches.

Engineering

One reference implementation in C++; Metal, CUDA and OpenCL kernels are line-by-line ports, and a parity harness drives the actual plugin GPU entry point — the one Resolve calls — asserting agreement with the reference to ~10⁻⁶ across 16 configurations. Even the film grain is bit-deterministic (integer-hash value noise). Honest limitations: no motion-vector compensation and no neural net — that's what keeps it fast, predictable, and free.

Get it

Install in a minute.

  1. macOS: download the .pkg from the latest release and double-click (signed with an Apple developer ID — if Gatekeeper objects, right-click → Open). Windows: download the zip, then double-click Install OpenNR (Windows).bat — or just drag OpenNR.ofx.bundle into C:\Program Files\Common Files\OFX\Plugins.
  2. Restart DaVinci Resolve.
  3. Find it under Effects → OpenFX → Filters → Hush → Hush Open NR. Drag onto an early node on the Color page (or a clip on the Edit page).
  4. Set Strength, check Step 5 → Noise Analysis to see the measurements, and — for tricky footage — drag the yellow sampling rectangle right in the viewer.

Works in the free version of Resolve (third-party OpenFX has been supported there since v15). The Windows build renders via OpenCL (NVIDIA, AMD and Intel GPUs); Linux builds from source.
