Why I built this

Of everything DaVinci Resolve locks behind the Studio paywall, noise reduction is the one that hits filmmakers hardest.

You can edit in the free version. You can grade in it. But the moment you shoot in low light — which is to say, the moment you make an independent film — you hit a wall: the Noise Reduction palette is Studio-only. And noise isn't a cosmetic problem you can defer. When you're doing color correction or VFX, a clean foundation is essential: every contrast move amplifies grain, every key edges against it, every composite inherits it. Denoising is infrastructure, not garnish.

Here's the thing: you don't need AI for this. The mathematics of good noise reduction — robust noise estimation, motion-gated temporal averaging, patch-based spatial filtering — has been understood for years. What it takes is a little cleverness, careful math, and the will to not charge for it.

OpenNR gets you noise reduction in the same class as Resolve Studio's built-in tool, for free — and goes one further. Resolve's NR is a black box: sliders in, pixels out. OpenNR is built around transparency. It measures your footage's noise and shows you the measurement. It shows you exactly what it's removing. It shows you where each stage is working. More insight into the process, more control over your final image.

Transparency is the feature

OpenFX plugins can't draw charts in Resolve's Inspector panel — so OpenNR turns the viewer itself into the scope. The same GPU kernels that filter your image render the measurement data into it on demand, which means the display can never disagree with what the filter is actually doing.

The UI is a lesson plan

The controls are organized as numbered steps that mirror the actual pipeline — because a tool this consequential to your image shouldn't require faith:

  1. Measure the noise. Automatic per-frame profiling, profiling from a region you place on a flat surface, or fully manual entry. An adjustment slider lets you overrule the machine.
  2. Temporal NR (across frames). The most effective, detail-safe reduction — with its own enable toggle so you can see exactly what it contributes.
  3. Spatial NR (within the frame). Cleans what remains, separately for brightness and color, with an edge-aware detail guard. Also independently toggleable.
  4. Inspect & compare. Split view, Noise Only view (what's being removed — if you can see your subject in it, you're cutting into the image), the Analysis HUD, and the Temporal Activity map.

Every control's tooltip answers three questions: what does this do, when should I touch it, and which direction do I push it. The defaults are calibrated from the measured noise, so the starting point is already fitted to your footage.

The technical detail

1 · Measuring noise without a test chart

Everything downstream is calibrated in units of the noise standard deviation σ, so measuring it well is the whole game. OpenNR runs three estimators per frame, all built on order statistics (medians and quantiles) so that edges, texture and motion fall in the tail of the distribution instead of biasing the estimate:

Temporal difference (primary). For video, the cleanest measurement of noise is the frame you just watched: on static pixels, the difference between two frames is pure noise with spread √2·σ — regardless of how spatially correlated the noise is. That correlation immunity matters, because debayering, chroma subsampling and compression smear real camera noise into blobs that fool detail-based estimators.

σ_T = median(|I_t − I_t±1|) / (0.6745 · √2)

Motion robustness: the unbiased median and 20th-percentile estimates are compared — on static footage they agree; motion inflates the median first, in which case only the static quantile is trusted. If even that vastly exceeds the spatial estimators, the whole frame is moving and the temporal signal is discarded.

Spatial Laplacian, two scales (floor). Immerkær's operator [1 −2 1; −2 4 −2; 1 −2 1] has response std 6σ on iid noise and rejects linear gradients; the median of |response| gives a robust fine-scale estimate. The same measurement on 2×2 block means catches spatially correlated noise the fine scale misses (for iid noise the two agree; correlated noise elevates the coarse one).

σ_fine = median(|L ∗ I|) / (6 · 0.6745) σ_coarse = 2 · median(|L ∗ blockmean₂(I)|) / (6 · 0.6745) σ_S = max(σ_fine, 0.9·σ_coarse, 0.85·σ_T)

Implementation: a strided GPU pass accumulates six 256-bin histograms with atomics; a single-thread pass extracts quantiles. Costs about 2% of the frame budget. The luma histogram and its median are what the Analysis HUD draws — the scope displays the literal data the filters consume.

2 · Temporal NR with a hard knee

Each pixel is compared patch-wise (3×3 mean absolute difference, computed separately for luma and chroma) against the same position up to two frames away. The expected difference for pure noise is 2σ/√π ≈ 1.13σ — below that, blending is free. The gate then falls to exactly zero over a user-scaled span:

w = 1 − smoothstep(1.13·σ_T, 1.13·σ_T + k·σ_T, |Δpatch|) k = motion threshold

The hard zero is the point. An early version used a Gaussian falloff, and its fat tail blended pixels several σ apart at 30–40% weight — invisible in fast-motion tests, but on real slow pans and soft gradients it produced exactly the ghosting soup you'd expect. With the knee, mismatched pixels can't bleed in by construction; chroma is additionally slaved to the luma gate so color can never trail on its own. The per-pixel effective sample count (Σw)²/Σw² is carried forward — it's what the Temporal Activity view visualizes, and it tells the spatial stage how much noise is already gone: σ_eff = σ_S/√effN.

3 · Spatial NR that knows the noise floor

Non-local means over a ±1…5 px window: each pixel is averaged only with neighbors whose surrounding 3×3 patches genuinely match. Patch distances are bias-corrected by the 2σ² that noise alone contributes, and the filter strength is tied to the measured noise — h = 1.15·σ_eff for luma, 2.0·σ_eff for chroma — so "how much to smooth" is a measurement, not a guess. Chroma weights are luma-guided (color can't bleed across a brightness edge), an edge-aware term shrinks h where local variance rises above the noise floor (the Preserve Detail control), and a bilateral mode exists for weaker GPUs.

Engineering: one algorithm, four implementations, proven equal

The reference implementation is a single C++ header. The Metal, CUDA and OpenCL kernels are line-by-line ports of it, and a parity harness calls the actual plugin GPU entry point — the one Resolve calls — across twelve configurations and asserts agreement with the reference to ~2×10⁻⁵. The algorithm suite itself runs on synthetic scenes with known injected noise: estimator accuracy on iid and correlated noise, PSNR gates for static, fast-motion and slow-drift cases (the slow-drift test exists specifically to make ghosting a regression failure), identity at zero strength, and rendered checks of every analysis view. All of it runs in CI, no Resolve required.

Honest limitations

No motion-compensated temporal NR (Studio tracks motion vectors and averages along them; OpenNR detects motion and protects it, handing those pixels to the spatial stage) and no neural network (Studio 20's UltraNR is genuinely impressive on extreme footage). On locked-off and normally-noisy material the results are comparable; those are the trade-offs that keep this fast, predictable, and free.

Install

  1. Grab OpenNR-2.0.0-macOS.pkg from the latest release and double-click it. The plugin is code-signed with my Apple developer ID; if Gatekeeper still objects to the installer, right-click → Open, or approve it under System Settings → Privacy & Security.
  2. Restart DaVinci Resolve.
  3. Find it under Effects → OpenFX → Filters → OpenNR → OpenNR Denoise — drag onto a node on the Color page (recommended: an early, pre-grade node) or onto a clip on the Edit page.
  4. Adjust Strength, then open Step 5 → Noise Analysis to see what it measured, and Noise Removed to see what it's removing. Finish with Step 4: shadow desaturation, texture, or film grain.
Works in the free version of Resolve — third-party OpenFX has been supported there since Resolve 15. Windows and Linux build from source today (CUDA/OpenCL backends are in the tree); prebuilt binaries are on the roadmap.
